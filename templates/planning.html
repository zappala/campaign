Planning

Summary

  • The city will be updating its parks plan this year to determine what our last few parks should contain and how the city should fund them.
  • The city likewise needs a plan for developing our commercial area that preserves green space, since the city-owned land was purchased with recreation impact fees. A splash pad is one possibility.
  • We need to decide how best to work with neighboring cities on improved police and fire services.
  • The city needs to pay for installing pressurized irrigation meters at each home, because dry summers and water shortages are forecast to continue. Pricing water usage is the most reasonable way to ensure our scarce resources are allocated efficiently.
  • There has been some preliminary discussion of a county-wide library system. The city should be planning on how it can be involved if such a system becomes a reality.

Parks

The General Plan has not been updated since 2002, and is particularly deficient with regard to parks. There is currently no funding for finishing the Bayhill Park, and the plan for this park may need to be modified in light of the new Murdock Canal Trail. The Harvey Parcel, slated to be a sports park, is currently still in litigation. The overall plan for parks needs to be updated, with citizen input on the facilities to build at each park and a plan for obtaining the funding necessary. Funding options include selling existing city property, such as the commercial land on 4800 W and the Cottonwood lots, bonding, and saving money for park construction each year.

Commercial Development

The city has been approached by a commercial developer who is interested in bringing a large development to Cedar Hills that will use the rest of our available commercial property. Tentative plans include a senior living facility, retail shops, restaurants, and a movie theater.

The city needs to plan carefully with this development, to ensure it fits with the surrounding community. Please see my blog at cedarhillsblog.org for more details.

In addition to these concerns, we need to be sure to preserve some green space in the area, perhaps with a splash pad. The city-owned land south of Hart's Gas Station was purchased with recreation impact fees, so I feel it is important we maintain some kind of recreational area there. We can also use revenue from selling or leasing city-owned land to acquire or develop future park land, such as the Harvey parcel or Bayhill Park.

As with any development, the city should continue to maintain its architectural standards and ensure resident concerns regarding light, noise, and traffic are addressed.

Public Safety

Photo courtesy Trent Augustus

The city has a number of options for improving our police and fire services. Currently we receive fire service from Lone Peak Public Safety District, police service from American Fork, and 911 dispatch from Utah Valley Dispatch. The city is considering options for increased fire staffing to improve response time when multiple calls occur simultaneously, and is also considering options for police services that could enhance service levels.

The issue is somewhat complex, because there are many moving pieces at the moment. (1) The Lone Peak Public Safety District is considering forming a taxing district, so that property taxes could be collected directly by the district rather than the participating cities. This would help provide improved transparency for residents and ease pressure on the city's budget when fees for fire coverage increase. (2) The Public Safety District is also considering switching to Pleasant Grove for Dispatch. If Cedar Hills continues using American Fork for police services, this would mean police dispatch would use Utah Valley and fire dispatch would use Pleasant Grove, which would require extra effort to ensure smooth operation. (3) There have been initial discussions on merging the fire departments of Lone Peak and several nearby cities. The city has also had preliminary discussions about using Lone Peak or Pleasant Grove for both police and fire services.

Ultimately, the city will need to navigate through these choices. Our primary concerns are providing high quality service while also keeping costs for these services reasonable. Our city has increased our budget for fire services significantly in recent years, in order to staff our own fire station and improve service times. We should not accept further cost increases unless this provides additional improvements in service. The city should also favor participation in a public safety district, as opposed to contracting with another city, since that provides us with a greater voice in how services are provided.

Pressurized Irrigation

One of the significant concerns of our public works department is ensuring that the city has sufficient water to meet our needs. In wet years, this is generally not a problem, but with our recent string of mild winters and dry and hot summers, water shortages have begun to be a problem in Utah County. If this trend continues, the city will need to be very careful about managing its water resources.

The best way to handle water conservation is through the market. Currently the city does not meter pressurized irrigation water, so the monthly fee is based on the size of your property, not how much water you actually use. By charging based on usage, we can use economic incentives to ensure we have adequate water to make it through very dry years. The major impediment to doing this is the installation cost, and city staff is exploring options to make this feasible.

Library

Photo courtesy Enokson on Flickr

Currently, Cedar Hills has a library reimbursement program so that residents can purchase library services from another city and receive back a portion of that fee (currently $30). Other local cities participate in the North Utah County Library Cooperative -- American Fork, Pleasant Grove, Lehi, Eagle Mountain and Highland.

There has been some preliminary discussion of expanding the North County cooperative system to include more cities, including Provo and Orem. Cedar Hills should position itself to be a part of any county library system. Currently the best way to do this would be to use a portion of the recreation center basement for a small library, including digital books. I am a strong supporter of a county-wide or even state-wide library system. By being part of a larger system, our residents would have access to a much larger supply of books; this is particularly important for our children as we encourage them to be frequent readers. We should be sure to use our bi-annual survey and resident meetings to gather feedback on whether this is something residents want to be a part of.
